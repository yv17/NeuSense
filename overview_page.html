Auditory illusions, also known as auditory pareidolia, occur when misperceiving real acoustic stimuli.
This definition creates a distinction from auditory hallucinations, which are perceived in the absence of a stimulus.
Auditory illusions allow us to hear sounds which are not present in the stimulus,
similar to how optical illusions allow us to see objects or shapes that are not present in an image.
One such example is the Ascending Shepard Scale.
Here, the listener hears a series of notes continuously rising in pitch.
However, this should not be possible, as a tone cannot increase in pitch infinitely,
and beyond the human hearing range, the listener should not be able to hear any sound.
